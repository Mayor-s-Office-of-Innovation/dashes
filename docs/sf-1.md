# How Municipalities Segment and Serve Different Personas of People With Substance Use Disorder on the Street

*Research brief — compiled June 23, 2026*

## Research question

How are different municipalities handling the different personas of people who are unhoused or on the streets with a substance use disorder — even if they may have housing? Starting example: [Houston's veterans-first, Housing First approach](https://www.seattletimes.com/seattle-news/homeless/houstons-solution-to-the-homeless-crisis-housing-and-lots-of-it) (Seattle Times).

---

## Summary

The dominant pattern is that cities don't treat "people on the street with a substance use disorder" as one population — they deliberately segment into personas and build a differentiated response for each. There are two layers: a *system* framework for slicing the population, and a *menu* of intervention models matched to specific personas.

The field is split between a **Housing First + harm reduction** pole (Houston, classic SF, Seattle LEAD, 1811 Eastlake) and a **Recovery First + accountability** pole (San Francisco 2025, sober transitional housing, SB 43 / CARE Court), with **CalAIM contingency management and ACT/ICM** as clinical tools both poles can use.

---

## 1. The operating frame: segment, then serve one persona at a time

Houston is one instance of a broader methodology used in 100+ communities, usually branded **Built for Zero / "functional zero"** (Community Solutions). Communities adopt real-time, person-specific (by-name) data to know every person experiencing homelessness and target resources accordingly, then work population by population.

- Rockford, IL — one of the first to reach functional zero for both veterans and chronic homelessness — sorts everyone on its by-name list into **five populations: single adults, youth under 25, veterans, chronically homeless, and families with children**.
- The logic is that focusing on one sub-population first generates spillover benefits and creates a template you then apply to the next group — the veterans-first → chronic-next sequence Houston used.
- Cities typically start with **veterans** not because they are the largest group, but because veteran functional zero has a clean, fundable definition (fewer veterans homeless than the system can house in a single month) and a dedicated federal funding stream (VA / HUD-VASH), making it the easiest credibility-building "win."
- Portland / Multnomah County prioritized **chronically homeless adults** first because their voter-passed Supportive Housing Services measure named that as "Population A."

At the system level, substance use disorder cuts *across* all personas rather than being its own bucket.

## 2. The refinement most relevant to the SUD focus

UCSF's CASPEH study (largest representative US study of homelessness since the 1990s) recommends shifting coordinated entry from purely *ranking* people by vulnerability to **matching people to the appropriate service setting**, sometimes via clinical evaluation. It builds a sub-persona called **"complex behavioral health needs."**

Key figures that reshape the personas:

- **~48%** of homeless adults in California have at least one complex behavioral health need — meaning **half do not**, and that half mostly needs affordable housing and mainstream services, not specialized clinical infrastructure.
- Substance personas are **meth-dominant, not opioid-dominant**: 32% report regular methamphetamine use vs. 11% opioids and 3% cocaine; among regular drug users, 91% use meth, and 80% of regular opioid users also use meth. Meth has no medication equivalent to methadone/buprenorphine — the evidence-based response is contingency management.
- **Justice / institutional** persona: people with complex needs are twice as likely (27% vs 12%) to have entered homelessness directly from jail, prison, or inpatient treatment — making reentry handoffs a recurring intervention point.
- **Alcohol** is a distinct persona: 9% report heavy episodic drinking (highest among Latino, Native, and Black respondents) — needing sobering centers and managed-alcohol approaches, not drug treatment.

## 3. The menu of models cities are matching to personas

Roughly ordered from lowest-barrier to most coercive:

**Housing First + harm reduction (baseline).** Housing without sobriety preconditions. Houston's engine and San Francisco's longstanding default. Strong evidence base — Pathways to Housing, Canada's Chez Soi RCT (2,100+ people, three-quarters with co-occurring SUD), and Santa Clara's Welcome Home all showed 80–90% housing retention for chronically homeless people with complex behavioral health problems.

**Single-site "managed" / damp or wet housing.** Seattle's **1811 Eastlake** (DESC) is the canonical persona-specific facility: supportive housing for ~75 formerly homeless adults with chronic alcohol use disorders who were the heaviest users of publicly funded crisis services. No sobriety requirement; early evaluations showed roughly $4M/year savings. The model for the "chronic alcoholic, highest-utilizer" persona.

**Pre-booking diversion.** Seattle's **LEAD** (Law Enforcement Assisted Diversion) targets the justice-adjacent persona — people suspected of low-level drug or prostitution offenses are diverted at the point of arrest to a harm-reduction case manager instead of jail. Widely replicated.

**Intensive clinical services layered onto housing.** For the highest-acuity co-occurring persona — **ACT (Assertive Community Treatment)** and **ICM (Intensive Case Management)**: multidisciplinary teams delivering psychiatric care, medication management, and housing support in the community with a "whatever it takes" approach. In California this runs through Full Service Partnerships.

**Contingency management.** California is the first state to cover **CM as a Medicaid benefit** (CalAIM "Recovery Incentives") — the key tool for the meth/stimulant persona since no medication exists. Eligible Medi-Cal members do a structured 24-week outpatient program earning low-denomination gift-card incentives for negative drug tests, followed by 6+ months of recovery support. San Francisco is a participating county. Works for housed *and* unhoused people.

**Sobering centers, managed alcohol, naltrexone.** UCSF recommends these as a *targeted* response for the alcohol-use-disorder persona, distinct from the drug-treatment track.

**Recovery-first / abstinence-based / sober transitional housing.** The significant 2025 shift in **San Francisco under Mayor Lurie**. The city signed "Recovery First" legislation enshrining long-term remission as SF's primary SUD policy goal, ended distribution of smoking supplies unless users enter treatment, and is standing up drug-free transitional housing (James Baldwin Place on 9th St, with the Salvation Army and Westside Community Services). Targets people who are largely recovered but still stabilizing — and if a participant relapses, they're sent to treatment rather than back to the street. A real departure from Housing First, and contested (advocates warn a narrow abstinence definition could gate people out of services).

**Compulsory / clinical-legal tools.** For the most severe, "gravely disabled" persona, California now has two coercive pathways: **CARE Court** (civil court-ordered care plans) and **SB 43**, which for the first time in 50+ years expanded conservatorship eligibility to include severe substance use disorder, not just mental illness. SF and counties phased these in through 2024–2026. Critics note no housing funds are attached to CARE Court and participants face long waits for bridge housing.

## 4. The "even if they have housing" thread

SUD doesn't resolve at the housing door — an underbuilt part of most systems:

- SUD is one of the most significant drivers of premature, negative exits from permanent supportive housing (eviction, return to homelessness), with disparities by race/ethnicity.
- UCSF recommends low-barrier treatment be available **at any point in the housing journey** — while housed, in shelter, or unsheltered — not gated to a particular status.
- The currently-housed-but-publicly-using persona is exactly who SF's new mobile triage/stabilization model targets: people picked up by police are taken to be placed into shelter or treatment, with jail as the alternative depending on their choices.
