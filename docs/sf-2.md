# San Francisco Substance Use: Methodology Notes & Three-Lens Data Breakdown

*Companion brief to "Municipal SUD Personas" research — compiled June 23, 2026*

This brief addresses a specific analytic problem: why statewide prevalence data (CASPEH) shows methamphetamine dominating, while San Francisco's streets and overdose deaths look opioid-dominated — and what San Francisco's own data actually says.

---

## What "contingency management" means (meth treatment context)

Contingency management (CM) is a behavioral treatment built on operant conditioning: patients earn tangible rewards — usually escalating low-value gift cards or vouchers — for objectively verified results, almost always a drug-negative urine test. The reward value climbs with each consecutive negative test and resets after a positive one.

It matters specifically for methamphetamine because, unlike opioids (methadone, buprenorphine) and alcohol (naltrexone), **there is no FDA-approved medication for stimulant use disorder**. CM is the single most evidence-backed treatment for meth and cocaine. In California's CalAIM "Recovery Incentives" version, it is a structured 24-week outpatient program with gift-card incentives for negative tests, followed by 6+ months of recovery support. The mechanism is to give the brain's reward system a competing, immediate, predictable reinforcer during the window when abstinence is hardest — not simply "paying people to quit."

## The core reframe: three different measures

What you see on the street, what a prevalence survey measures, and what kills people are three distinct quantities that can diverge widely and all be accurate:

1. **Regular-use prevalence** — self-reported use among the *living* population (e.g., CASPEH's 32% meth / 11% opioid, statewide, 2021–22).
2. **Visible public intoxication** — the forward-folded "nod" is opioid sedation; stationary, slumped, highly salient in public space.
3. **Overdose mortality** — in SF, fentanyl-dominated (71–81% of deaths in recent years).

Prevalence ≠ visibility ≠ lethality. A drug can be less prevalent but far more visible and far more lethal. Fentanyl is exactly that.

## Why the statewide CASPEH number doesn't describe SF

**Methodology of CASPEH (the statewide 32/11 figure):**

- Largest representative US study of homelessness since the mid-1990s; ~3,200 questionnaires + 365 in-depth interviews.
- Data collected **October 2021 – November 2022** (predates the deepest fentanyl penetration; SF fentanyl deaths peaked in 2023).
- **Statewide** estimate weighting eight counties across urban, rural, and suburban California. Meth dominates much of non-SF California; fentanyl is geographically concentrated, with SF a national epicenter. The statewide average dilutes SF's opioid concentration.
- Robust sampling design: combined venue-based (time-location) sampling with respondent-driven peer-referral sampling specifically to avoid the service-venue selection bias common in homelessness research. → The meth dominance is likely a *real* feature of the statewide population, not a sampling artifact.

**Why your street observation skews opioid (and is still valid):**

- Opioid intoxication = sedation, the forward "nod," stationary and slumped = extremely salient and photographable.
- Meth is a stimulant: users stay awake (CASPEH interviews describe using meth to stay awake and guard against theft/violence/sweeps), are mobile and dispersed, and may present as agitation/psychosis that observers file under "mental illness" rather than "drug use." → Casual visual sampling systematically over-counts opioids and under-counts meth.

**Directional biases that understate opioids in the statewide number relative to current SF:**

- **Temporal lag** — data is 3–4 years old; fentanyl penetration deepened afterward.
- **Survivorship** — a cross-sectional survey samples the living; fentanyl is acutely lethal, differentially removing heavy opioid users from the interviewable population (they appear in mortality data, not prevalence).
- **Supply opacity / self-report** — people often don't know their supply contains fentanyl; "non-prescribed opioids" depends on accurate self-knowledge. (Does not operate through the 3×/week threshold — anyone physically dependent uses far more than that.)

**Polysubstance overlap dissolves the dichotomy:** CASPEH found 80% of regular opioid users also use meth, and 91% of all regular drug users use meth. The 32/11 split counts indicators, not mutually exclusive people.

## San Francisco's own data — three lenses

Pulled from SFDPH and the Office of the Chief Medical Examiner (OCME). Note the SFDPH treatment-entry figures below are calendar-2020 / FY2020-21 (COVID-depressed) — the most granular public "primary substance at admission" statement found directly from SFDPH; see the "current information" follow-up for post-COVID updates.

### Lens 1 — Treatment entry (who enrolls)
- SFDPH states **opioids, methamphetamine, and alcohol** were the primary diagnoses of clients entering SUD treatment — not opioid-only.
- 2020: **6,179** individuals enrolled in SUD treatment; **70%** had experienced homelessness; **46%** also received a mental health service.
- Race: 42% white, 28% African-American, 20% Latino/a — Black and Latino/a clients disproportionate to population share.

### Lens 2 — Treatment infrastructure (what the system is built around)
- The public treatment dataset and funding are organized around **opioid medications**: methadone, buprenorphine, naloxone distribution, plus residential and withdrawal-management admissions.
- Opioid Treatment Programs served **2,732** clients, funded at ~**$20.6M** — one of the two largest service lines.
- **No parallel medication metric exists for meth** (no medication exists). Meth treatment = **contingency management**, delivered via SF AIDS Foundation's PROP and UCSF's STOP — recommended by a 2019 meth task force, initially targeted (gay/bi/trans men; co-occurring mental health + stimulant use), expanding from 2022. The 2024 Overdose Prevention Plan grew CM programs from **three to ten**.

### Lens 3 — Mortality (who dies)
- Fentanyl-dominant: **2023's record 806 overdose deaths included 653 from fentanyl**; fentanyl drove ~**75%** of deaths in 2025 and ~**71%** in 2024.
- Meth and cocaine appear heavily as **co-factors** (each present in roughly half of 2023 deaths) — polysubstance, not a separate population.

### Cross-cutting: most diagnosed people aren't in treatment
- Of **11,570** SF Health Network patients diagnosed with an SUD in 2020, **4,896 (42%)** enrolled in treatment — high by California county standards, but still leaving the majority untreated.

---

## Why this resolves the puzzle (and what it means for mapping)

In San Francisco specifically, opioids co-lead treatment entry *and* dominate mortality (71–81% fentanyl) — nothing like the 11% statewide figure. Meth is also major (co-leads treatment entry; present in ~half of deaths) but quieter for two structural reasons the data exposes: **no medication exists** (so it's invisible in opioid-centric treatment metrics) and **stimulant users are less visible** than the sedated opioid presentation. Alcohol is a third co-leader at the treatment door that's nearly invisible in the overdose-death framing.

**For SF resource mapping:** keep all three lenses side by side — treatment entry (opioid + meth + alcohol), treatment infrastructure (opioid-medication-heavy; meth covered only by CM), and mortality (fentanyl-dominant, polysubstance) — each labeled with what it measures and its year. The gaps between the three lenses are where the underservice lives, and the quiet, partly-housed, no-medication meth population is the one visual sampling will miss.
